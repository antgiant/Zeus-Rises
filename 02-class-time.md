# Chapter 2 – Class Time

“Professor!” John joyously exclaimed. “I have it! I have it right here!” as he waved a collection of crumpled printouts covered in handwritten notes over his head.

They don’t pay me enough for this thought Jane for the hundredth time as she grimly made her way down the hallway towards 105.

John rushed to meet her oblivious to everything and everyone between them, words tumbling out with abandon. “Here’s the proof! I knew it! I knew I was right! I looked it up just like you told me to, and it was all there! I knew you were just testing me! You said that Astrology isn’t part of Astronomy, and that it isn’t even part of science, but Astrology is the original Astronomy! Galileo, who you talked about inventing Astronomy last week, he was an Astrologer, and today you are talking about Kepler’s Laws! Kepler is the man in Astrology! His calendars are amazing! Here! Look! Kepler, Galileo it is all here!” And with that triumphant proclamation John shoved his handful of papers at Jane.

Jane looked at the papers with distain, then at John with pity. “It’s staring you right in the face and you can’t see it.” She said.

“Nothing is proved to be science by smart or important people believing it to be true. If that were true then Crazy Joe’s lunatic babbling down on the quad are science. After all the entire western world once believed, just like him, in a literal hell fire, a flood covering the entire planet and even the ludicrous idea of a star pointing out an individual house. Just because they all believed the same fairy tales doesn’t make them true. In fact, that is what separates science from the hocus pocus of religion. Science tests ideas. When they work, we scientists keep them, when they stop working, we throw them away. That’s why a country run by scientists is called enlightened and one run by the church lives in the dark ages.

Galileo and Kepler lived before Astrology was tested. Science has tested it since then, and it failed—miserably. So, today it lives on the rubbish heap of bygone ideas, right next to religion. This was staring you in the face the whole time. 

Look at the top page of what you're holding. That’s the Wikipedia page for Kepler, isn’t it? I thought I recognized it from the last confused student who made the same mistake you have. If you actually read it, you will discover that it says Kepler’s questions about the scientific underpinnings of Astrology helped kick start the real research that ended up proving Astrology to be nothing more than wishful thinking. Certainly not science. You’re a smart student John. Follow that trail and I have no doubt that you can verify this yourself. Just remember it is hard evidence not anecdotes or famous people’s opinions that make something real science.”

With that Jane spun and strode purposefully into the classroom. Something must have struck home to John, Jane thought. Because he did not interrupt once, and he spent the entire class lost to the world looking things up on his laptop. Whenever she noticed it, Jane would feel rising tension at the crazy she would inevitably have to listen to as a result of those searches. Then she would forcibly remind herself to breathe and not worry about future Jane’s problems, and continued class. Still, Jane was glad when she finally finished class and could finally head back to her office to work on her real job. Research.
